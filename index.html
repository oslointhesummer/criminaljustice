The Case for Public Defense Funding in the Bay Area

What is a "Public Defender"?

Public defenders are lawyers who represent criminal defendants who cannot afford to hire a lawyer.

This is a right guaranteed through the Sixth Amendment of the US Constitution.

Funding Priorities in United States

The US spends 57x more on police & 40x more on incarceration vs. public defense.

Our spending priorities create a system inclined towards arrest and imprisonment, but are we able to effectively defend the underprivileged?

Public Defenders are Underfunded and Overworked

The bar association recommends that public defenders who handle misdemeanor cases do not work more than 400 cases a year and for those who handle felony cases to not work more than 150 cases a year. Unfortunately, public defenders often needing to go over these limits. Here, you can see the caseloads for Alameda county.

Why Does Surveillance Matter?

Surveillance technologies disproportionately target low-income people and people of color, ultimately leading to increased policing of those communities. For example, a Bay Area advocacy group found that automated license plate readers in Oakland were primarily located in low-income minority neighborhoods, irrespective of crime rates in the area. Across the US, this pattern persists. One study found, for example, that after installing surveillance cameras in residential neighbrhoods in Michigan, African-American residents were twice as likely to be surveilled than white residents.

"Cutting-edge technologies are prone to be targeted at communities of color."

Race Distribution of Jailed Persons (1985-2018)

One outcome of biased policing and surveillance technologies used to target people of color is: a disproportionate racial make up of jails

For example, Black people make up 6% of California's population, but they represented 21% of the jail population in California in 2018

California Jail Pretrial Population & Bail

As of 2018, 64% of the jail population in California is pre-trial, meaning they are still waiting for their case to reach a judge.

However, this is contrast to the Sixth Amendment, which guarantees the right to a public trial without unnecessary delay

California's total jail population has increased by 175% from 1970-2018, in part this could be because of the prohibitive bail cost of bail and case load burden faced by public defenders

Is California's Corrections Department Spending Socially Progressive?

Between 2021-2022, California plans to spend $17 Billion dollars on its Corrections & Rehabilitation Department.

47% ($8 Billion) of this funding is spent on administrative oversight activities and operations.

Only 4% ($541 Million) is spent directly on rehabilitative services. Are these the spending priorities of a progressive system?

Conclusion

We need to reallocate funds from law enforcement to public defenders. While Bay Area law enforcement uses their vast funds to acquire surveillance technologies, which target low-income people and people of color, public defenders struggle to defend those same groups of people.
