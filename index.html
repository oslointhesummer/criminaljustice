The Case for Increasing Public Defense Funding in the Bay Area

Justice System Funding Priorities within Bay Area

Bay Area counties consistently spend less on public defense and more on law enforcement and detention and corrections.


Public Defenders are Underfunded and Overworked

The bar association recommends that public defenders who handle misdemeanor cases work no more than 400 cases a year and that those who handle felony cases work no more than 150 cases a year.


Unfortunately, public defenders often need to go over these limits. Here, you can see the caseloads for Alameda county. A misdemeanor is a less serious crime than a felony and carries lighter penalties. Thus, more time is required for public defenders to work on felony cases.

How Do Bay Area Law Enforcement Use Their Funding?
Often, for surveillance.

Bay Area law enforcement use their funding to acquire many surveillance technologies. Take a look at the percentage of Bay Area cities whose law enforcement use the following surveillance technologies.

In addition to state and county funding, Bay Area agencies receive millions of dollars of technology through federal grants. Between 2010 and 2020, the Bay Area received $11.9 million worth of technology through federal grants. The most frequently cited terms in federal grants to Bay Area law enforcement relate to surveillance (e.g. "sight," "vision," and "illuminator").

Disproportionate Race Distribution in California Jails (1985-2018)

One outcome of biased policing and surveillance technologies used to target people of color is: a disproportionate racial make up of jails.


For example, Black people make up 6% of California's population, but they represented 21% of the jail population in California in 2018.

California Jail Pretrial Population & Bail

As of 2018, 64% of the jail population in California is pre-trial, meaning they are still waiting for their case to reach a judge. However, this is in contrast to the Sixth Amendment, which guarantees the right to a public trial without unnecessary delay.


*Hover on top of the line chart to see population values

California's total jail population has increased by 175% from 1970-2018, in part this could be because of the prohibitive bail cost of bail and case load burden faced by public defenders.

Is California's Corrections Department Spending Socially Progressive?

Between 2021-2022, California plans to spend $13 Billion dollars on its Corrections & Rehabilitation Department.


68% ($9 Billion) of this funding is spent on administrative oversight activities and operations.

Only 4% ($541 Million) is spent directly on rehabilitative services. Are these the spending priorities of a progressive system?

Conclusion

We need to reallocate funds from law enforcement to public defenders. While Bay Area law enforcement uses their vast funds to acquire surveillance technologies, which target low-income people and people of color, public defenders struggle to defend those same groups of people.
